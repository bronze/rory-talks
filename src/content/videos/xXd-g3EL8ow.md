## Key Takeaways

- Most gains from rising productivity and dual-income households have been absorbed by rising property prices, leaving younger generations no wealthier in real terms despite working harder and longer than their parents.
- Property is "an appalling way to store money": it is illiquid, sentimentally distorting, and has been made effectively tax-free in ways that create a massive intergenerational wealth transfer.
- Government lacks a marketing function — it has press relations and communications, but no mechanism for finding out through deliberative democracy what actually makes people's daily lives better, which is often potholes rather than grand strategy.
- Politicians trained to stay on message and never cross a line become uniformly unconvincing; the politician who deviates, even awkwardly, is immediately more interesting because authenticity is extremely hard to fake at scale.
- Bureaucratic friction — slow planning permission, complex tax rules, impenetrable bank account opening — has a larger opportunity cost than its direct costs suggest, because the real damage is the things that never happen as a result.
- Social spending on pubs, companionship, and shared public spaces deserves the same policy attention as diet; loneliness has a measurable impact on health and longevity comparable to smoking, yet receives a tiny fraction of the policy discussion.
