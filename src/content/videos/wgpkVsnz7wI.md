## Key Takeaways

- Utility in economics is assumed to be additive — you net out good and bad — but real life is multiplicative: one catastrophic zero wipes out everything else, which is why reputation works the way it does.
- Choice reduction is often a feature, not a bug: TikTok and Tesla succeed partly because they constrain the degrees of freedom enough that users can't produce a bad outcome, unlike systems that hand you infinite rope.
- The paradox of choice is real but subtle — it's not just about too many options, it's about being forced to make micro-decisions on things you resent having to think about, like paying separately for fog lamps on a £70,000 car.
- Trump voting is entirely rational once you reframe it: a boss who is slightly incompetent but genuinely likes you is preferable to a highly competent one who secretly despises you — the technocratic elite finds this incomprehensible because they've never had to make that choice.
- Physical objects and printed information carry a trust signal that digital systems don't: a laminated taxi tariff card is credible precisely because the driver can't change it per passenger, whereas a digital display removes that guarantee.
