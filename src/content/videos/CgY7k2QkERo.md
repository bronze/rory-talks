## Key Takeaways

- Marketing is a casino with the odds stacked in your favour — but pretending it's a Newtonian science with predictable, measurable outputs has painted the industry into a corner where only bottom-of-funnel, easily quantifiable work gets approved.
- Fame is a probability amplifier: the more famous a brand or person is, the more surface area they have for lucky accidents — viral moments, word-of-mouth, and inbound opportunities that obscure brands simply never encounter.
- The entrepreneurial mindset treats counterintuitive data as an arbitrage opportunity; the bureaucratic mindset treats it as a threat to the model — this difference in disposition explains why large organisations systematically under-invest in exploration.
- Bezos's two-way door framework is the missing rule in marketing decision-making: demanding the same level of rigour for reversible experiments as for irreversible capital commitments is a category error that disguises ass-covering as rigour.
- AI is most valuable as a stimulus or advisor that hands off to human judgement, and most dangerous when it removes the human entirely — because institutions will rationally choose to follow an AI recommendation to avoid blame even when their own judgement says otherwise.
- The Jaguar rebrand may be defensible: when electrification resets the rules of a category entirely, brand equity built around one set of customers can become an anchor, and sometimes you have to piss off your existing customers to survive.
