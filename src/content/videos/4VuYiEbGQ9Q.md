## Key Takeaways

- Economics has gained an unwarranted monopoly over business and policy decision-making by building elegant mathematical models that assume rational individual agents — ignoring habits, social contagion, and the fact that people are influenced by others.
- Wine's popularity is largely a social construct: it comes in a convenient binary choice (red/white), has no known price anchor allowing egregious mark-ups, and social embarrassment stops people from complaining when it's bad.
- Network effects explain why the English are bad at learning foreign languages: the gain to a Dutchman learning English is enormous, while the gain to an Englishman learning Dutch only kicks in after an almost impossible level of fluency.
- Behaviour change policies designed around individual rational agents fail because they don't become socially contagious; binary rules (like a Sabbath) work far better than quantitative limits (like "21 units a week") because they're enforceable at the social level.
- The Minnesota Zipper Merge shows how simply naming a behaviour creates a social norm that can change traffic flow more effectively than compulsion.
- Small, named heuristics — like "designated driver" or "Bob" — are more powerful tools for behaviour change than incentives or legislation because they make the behaviour cognitively easy and socially self-reinforcing.
