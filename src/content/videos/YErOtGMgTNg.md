## Key Takeaways

- When products succeed we forget how decisive marketing was; Steve Jobs was not primarily a technologist but an extraordinary pitch man and marketer who made the product feel inevitable.
- If you can imagine a stand-up comedian doing a routine about your product, you are on to something — the urge to appear serious is a disaster in marketing because it trades distinctiveness for respectability.
- Idiosyncrasies count double: preserve the odd, distinctive, slightly inexplicable features of your product or brand rather than rationalising them away, because they are often what makes you memorable.
- The Facebook TV Portal was genuinely excellent but was destroyed in reviews by nine paragraphs of privacy paranoia before a single line praising its quality — showing how context and framing can kill a good product.
- Brand fame creates an escape velocity: when you are not famous you must find every customer yourself; once you reach a threshold of fame, customers start coming to you, transforming the economics of growth.
- Consistency, distinctiveness, and fame are the three fundamentals of early-stage brand building — without fame in particular, you have to rediscover your audience repeatedly rather than compounding recognition over time.
