## Key Takeaways

- Progress more often comes from *seeing* things differently than from doing them better — reframing is a form of innovation that costs almost nothing yet can be worth billions, as Adam Smith intuited two centuries before behavioural economics had a name.
- How information is framed is as real as the information itself: expressing speed as "time per distance" rather than "distance per time" physically changes how drivers respond — a reminder that the presentation of data is never neutral.
- The division of labour is Smith's most cited insight and his most misapplied one — he explicitly warned that reducing a person to a few repetitive operations destroys their intelligence, and that the principle breaks down in fields where human judgement, tacit knowledge, and context matter.
- Efficiency models follow a predictable arc from *toy* to *theory* to *tradition* to *trap*: what begins as a useful tool hardens into unquestioned ideology, engineers out all resilience and nuance, and eventually collapses under Goodhart's Law — any metric that becomes a target loses its value as a metric.
- The farmers' market is an economic abomination that exposes a blind spot in mainstream economics: people sometimes *want* to pay more, wait longer, and talk to a stranger, because what they are actually purchasing is intimacy and street theatre, not calories.
- Boulton and Watt's real genius was not the steam engine but the business model — charging mine owners one-third of their coal savings rather than upfront capital, and inventing "horsepower" as a unit so customers could do the ROI calculation themselves on the back of an envelope.

## Stories & Examples

- **The paceometer** — [4:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=290) Israeli researchers reframe speed as time-per-distance rather than distance-per-time. Reveals that at 70mph, accelerating to 80 saves almost nothing — but going from 3mph to 15mph on a bicycle halves a four-hour commute.
- **The London Overground** — [9:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=577) Silverlink Metro, a ghost train with tumbleweeds on the platforms, is rebranded as a tube line and added to the tube map. Now carries as many passengers per day as the £20bn Elizabeth line — for £200m plus ink and pixels.
- **Bottomless brunch** — [11:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=703) "A breakfast for alcoholics" reframed with prosecco becomes bottomless brunch: completely acceptable morning drinking. The idea is identical; only the presentation changed.
- **Range anxiety and electric cars** — [12:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=735) Billions spent increasing EV range when reducing anxiety was the real job. In the UK — which is "very, very small" — range anxiety consists of "oh, that charger isn't working, oh, there's one over there."
- **Steve Jobs and the iMac** — [12:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=735) Everyone optimised what a computer could do; Jobs asked what it feels like while you're doing it. Before the iMac, putting a computer in any room turned it into an office.
- **Uber** — [15:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=946) Didn't make taxis faster or cheaper. Changed the quality of waiting by showing a moving taxi on a map. Even if the map is lying, you feel better when there's a sign of progress.
- **Brazilian jiu-jitsu** — [16:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=988) Every martial art teaches standing combat — 3,000 years of optimisation left no gaps. One Brazilian inventor asked what happens when you're already on the ground, built an entirely new discipline around that axis, then proved it by challenging other martial artists.
- **English landscape gardening** — [17:48](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=1068) Capability Brown and others rejected French chess-board symmetry for idealised natural landscape — one of Britain's most underrated artistic achievements, and one that happened to peak around 1776.
- **The Gentleman Farmer** — [19:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=1146) A book rebranding peasant agriculture as a respectable pursuit for high-status people. George III read and reread it, became "Farmer George," and was widely ridiculed — though Rory argues he would have been great friends with Franklin and Jefferson.
- **Boulton & Watt — horsepower** — [20:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=1245) Couldn't sell steam engines by talking about cylinder size and calorific capacity. Invented "horsepower" so mine owners could do the ROI calculation on the back of an envelope: "Buy a 20hp engine, get rid of 60 horses — they don't need to rest, sleep, or be fed."
- **Boulton & Watt — hardware as a service** — [23:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=1395) Charged customers one-third of their coal savings per year rather than upfront capital. Installed first in Cornwall where coal was expensive, perfectly aligning incentives. Rolls-Royce Aero rediscovered the same model eight years ago as "power by the hour."
- **Haydn's IP rights** — [25:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=1507) In 1776 Haydn negotiated the right to sell his music directly, having previously been owned entirely by the Esterházy family. Probably explains why he went on to write approximately 103 symphonies.
- **The American Declaration of Independence** — [25:45](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=1545) Argued as the greatest ever use of the "woke playbook": a litany of confected grievances masking real motives. John Hancock's Boston Tea Party protected his smuggling business; Robert Morris's bankrolling of the war freed his shipping company from British trading rules; Washington's real grievance was land capture west of the Appalachians. Dr Johnson spotted it immediately: "the loudest yelps for liberty among the drivers of negroes."
- **The Cornwall delivery driver** — [36:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=2176) A former DPD driver with deep local knowledge rewrote his algorithmic route plan every morning. Knocked off at 2:30 while colleagues worked until 5. Illustration of what's lost when algorithms replace tacit knowledge.
- **The Doorman Fallacy** — [36:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=2176) Consultants define a hotel doorman's job as "opening doors," replace him with an automatic door, and claim the savings — ignoring everything else he actually did. The template for redefining human jobs narrowly enough to automate them.
- **Adam Smith's lazy boy (the potter cord)** — [37:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=2261) A boy paid to open and close a steam engine flap noticed a moving part in exact synchronicity with his action, tied them together with string, and went off to play. Smith celebrated it as worker-driven innovation. Rory notes the boy had actually automated himself out of a job.
- **Schiphol Airport** — [45:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=2751) Expanded as a single terminal to preserve maximum efficiency. Now barely usable — if you're on the BA flight to London City from gate 107B, "you're practically in Belgium by the time you board."
- **Tesco Extra and self-checkout** — [45:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=2751) Self-checkouts drove mass shoplifting — shops discovered they were selling more onions than they were buying, and avocados were being checked out as carrots. Consultants claimed the cost savings but were never held responsible for the losses that followed.
- **The farmers' market Zoom call** — [49:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=2942) During COVID, a Chicago economics graduate convened Daniel Kahneman, Richard Thaler, and others to solve the problem of leftover vegetables. Two said drop the price. Kahneman said farmers' markets are street theatre — if anything, raise the price. Rory's answer: hide it in the van and give it to your best customer, because the relationship is worth more than the cauliflower.
- **The toy-to-trap arc** — [53:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=3206) Guru Madavan's four stages of efficiency models: toy (useful operational tool), theory (extrapolated into ideology), tradition (hardened into ritual and bonus structures), trap (Goodhart's Law kicks in and the system collapses).
- **The Meta petrol tanker** — [55:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=3340) Visiting Menlo Park, Rory sees a miniature fuel tanker filling up coders' cars in the Meta car park so they don't have to stop on the way home. His "epiphany of terror": the most left-brain approach to optimising a life imaginable — never mind that normal humans combine a petrol stop with buying a pork pie and having a chat.
- **Buying a new car as philanthropy** — [60:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=md9UAorQ_B0&t=3604) Reframes car depreciation: buying a new Rolls-Royce Spectre donates a very nice car to someone poorer three years later. Nobody does that with houses. Property price rises reported as good news is "like saying great news for people with a full petrol tank — the price of fuel is going up."
