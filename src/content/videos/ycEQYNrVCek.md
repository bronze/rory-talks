## Key Takeaways

- Mike Smith's real mistake in rejecting the Beatles wasn't choosing the wrong band — it was accepting an artificial either/or constraint; signing both was the obvious right answer, made invisible by a scarcity mindset.
- Costs are immediate and quantifiable; opportunity costs are not — this asymmetry systematically biases decisions toward short-term efficiency and away from long-term value.
- The pretence of rationality carries a hidden cost: it demands a single right answer, ruling out the most creative solutions which often involve doing two seemingly contradictory things at once.
- Soft power — shaping behaviour through context, culture, and design rather than enforcement — becomes disproportionately valuable precisely when the world becomes more coercive and rule-bound.
- Framing every decision as either/or is arbitrary but feels natural; recognising this bias is the first step toward genuinely better choices.
