## Key Takeaways

- Remote working changed the subjective experience of commuting: once you have experienced rolling out of bed and clicking "join meeting", the commute that previously felt normal suddenly feels twice as bad — demonstrating that reference points, not absolute conditions, drive satisfaction.
- New behaviours are sticky once adopted: people who switch to electric cars, automatic transmission, or Quooker taps almost never revert, which means the real marketing challenge is getting people to try, not to stay.
- Hybrid working needs to be designed around the specific value of physical presence — serendipity, coaching, collaboration — rather than treating the office as the default and remote as the exception.
- Early technology adopters often pay a status tax: they buy unreliable, expensive products not for utility but to be the first person on their street with one, and in doing so they fund the development that eventually benefits everyone.
- The music and commuting industries both illustrate how people accept suboptimal arrangements simply because they have no comparison point, and how rapidly attitudes shift once an alternative is experienced.
- Secretive salary information in companies is a deliberate control mechanism: informal in-person conversation between colleagues reveals pay disparities that digital-only communication would never surface, making the office politically inconvenient for employers.
