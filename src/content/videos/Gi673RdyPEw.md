## Key Takeaways

- Humans are a *rationalising* species, not a rational one — we make instinctive decisions and reverse-engineer plausible explanations afterwards; the conscious brain is the press office, not the Oval Office.
- Consumers have no sense of proportion and neither do marketers — a £25 phone holder on a £55m aircraft can be the most memorable part of the journey, because perception is relative and context-dependent, not linear.
- Big data aggregates and averages away the outliers that actually matter: all big data comes from the past, yet important signals about the future arrive first in anecdotal form, as the Titanic's iceberg illustrated.
- The opposite of a good idea can be another good idea — getting people to walk more may require not just more footpaths but more benches, because people walk further when they know they can sit down.
- Marketing is the science of knowing what economists are wrong about: price is not simply a number that determines demand — framing, context, and signalling mean raising a price can sometimes increase it.
- The problem with winning arguments is that it requires starting from a reasonable assumption and deducing a single right answer, whereas problem-solving demands sitting with contradictions, outliers, and information that has no evidential value yet.
