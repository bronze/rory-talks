## Key Takeaways

- The power of reframing cannot be overstated: standing alone staring out of a window makes you an antisocial oddity, but doing the same thing with a cigarette makes you a philosopher — the activity is identical, the meaning is entirely different.
- A pensioner and a young unemployed person are in objectively similar circumstances, yet the pensioner is happy and the unemployed person is depressed; the difference is perceived control and the story told about the situation.
- Branded painkillers measurably reduce pain more than unbranded ones — perception is not just a filter on reality, it leaks back and changes the physical experience itself.
- The frame around a financial transaction transforms its emotional meaning: paying a toll that funds a charity feels acceptable; the same payment going to the toll company feels like extortion.
- Economics treats money as fungible, but to real humans £10 paid in one context is not the same as £10 paid in another — the meaning of a cost depends enormously on where it appears to go.
- Psychological and engineering solutions should be given equal consideration when solving problems; the consistent over-investment in objective, quantifiable interventions and under-investment in subjective ones is a structural bias rather than a rational choice.
